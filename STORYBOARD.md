## NARRATIVE ARC

**Central thesis**: LLMs are not programmed — they are *grown*. Intelligence emerges from vectors transforming through layers, shaped by optimization on human text.

**Metaphor that threads through**: A seed (random weights) planted in soil (data), grown via sunlight (gradient descent), pruned by gardeners (RLHF), and eventually producing fruit (useful outputs) — but fruit with unexpected shapes (jagged intelligence).

## ACT 2: THE MACHINERY — How Transformers Process Meaning (~13 min, Slides 13-23)

- **Animation Step 4**: Show arrows indicating "this block repeats N times" (e.g., 96 times for GPT-4)
- **Key point**: "Every LLM is just this pattern repeated many times: Attention → MLP → Attention → MLP → ..."
- **Citation**: Vaswani et al., "Attention Is All You Need," 2017

### Slide 14: The Residual Stream — Information Highway
- **Content**: "Vectors flow through the model like water through a pipe. Each layer reads from and writes to this stream."
- **Visual**: Horizontal stream flowing left to right, with attention and MLP blocks reaching in to read/modify
- **Animation Step 1**: Show a vector flowing through the residual stream
- **Animation Step 2**: An attention head reaches in, reads the vector, and adds information back
- **Animation Step 3**: An MLP block reads and adds more information
- **Animation Step 4**: Show the vector has been enriched at each step
- **Key metaphor**: "Think of it as a river. Each layer adds to the water; nothing is replaced entirely."
- **Technical detail**: "x = x + Attention(x), then x = x + MLP(x) — residual connections"

### Slide 15: Attention — The Core Innovation (Intuition)
- **Content**: "Attention answers the question: 'Which other words should I pay attention to when understanding THIS word?'"
- **Visual**: Sentence "The cat sat on the mat because it was tired" with attention lines
- **Animation Step 1**: Highlight "it" — what does "it" refer to?
- **Animation Step 2**: Draw attention lines from "it" to every other word, varying thickness
- **Animation Step 3**: The thickest line connects "it" to "cat" — the model learned this!
- **Animation Step 4**: Show another head where "tired" attends strongly to "cat"
- **Quote**: *"Self-attention allows the model to associate 'it' with 'animal'"* — Jay Alammar
- **Key insight**: "Attention is learned, not programmed. The model discovers these relationships from data."

### Slide 16: Query, Key, Value — The QKV Mechanism
- **Content**: "How attention works mechanically: every token produces three vectors"
- **Visual**: Three colored vectors emerging from each token
- **Animation Step 1**: Show a token. Three matrices (Wq, Wk, Wv) transform it into Q, K, V
- **Animation Step 2**: Q = "What am I looking for?" (query)
- **Animation Step 3**: K = "What do I contain?" (key — like a label)
- **Animation Step 4**: V = "What information do I actually carry?" (value — the content)
- **Animation Step 5**: Animate the dot product: Q·K = attention score (how well does my question match your label?)
- **Animation Step 6**: High score → pull in more of that token's Value
- **Analogy**: "Like a library: Q = your question, K = book titles, V = book contents. You find the best matching titles and read those books."

### Slide 17: Attention Scores → Softmax → Weighted Sum
- **Content**: "The mathematical steps of attention"
- **Visual**: Matrix heatmap showing attention scores
- **Animation Step 1**: Grid of Q·K scores (raw numbers)
- **Animation Step 2**: Divide by √d_k (scaling — prevents extreme values)
- **Animation Step 3**: Apply softmax — scores become probabilities (row sums to 1)
- **Animation Step 4**: Color-coded heatmap showing which tokens attend to which
- **Animation Step 5**: Weighted sum of V vectors using these attention weights → new representation
- **Key formula**: Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QK^T / √d_k) · V (show visually, not just math)

### Slide 18: Multi-Head Attention — Multiple Perspectives
- **Content**: "The model doesn't just look at relationships one way — it uses multiple 'heads' to look at different types of relationships simultaneously"
- **Visual**: 8 parallel attention operations, each with different color
- **Animation Step 1**: Show one attention head highlighting syntax (subject-verb relationships)
- **Animation Step 2**: Another head highlighting coreference (pronoun-noun relationships)
- **Animation Step 3**: Another head highlighting position (nearby words)
- **Animation Step 4**: All 8 heads shown in parallel, outputs concatenated
- **Key point**: "GPT-4 has 128 attention heads per layer, across 120+ layers"

### Slide 19: The MLP — Processing and Remembering
- **Content**: "After attention gathers context, the MLP (feed-forward network) processes it — this is where much of the model's 'knowledge' is stored"
- **Visual**: Simple two-layer network with expansion and contraction
- **Animation Step 1**: Input vector (d dimensions)
- **Animation Step 2**: First linear layer expands to 4d dimensions (unlock more capacity)
- **Animation Step 3**: Non-linear activation (GELU) — this is where the "thinking" happens
- **Animation Step 4**: Second linear layer compresses back to d dimensions
- **Key insight**: "The MLP is like a lookup table of patterns. Attention routes the right information; the MLP processes it."

### Slide 20: How Representations Transform Layer by Layer
- **Content**: "As information flows through layers, the vectors change — from surface features to deep understanding"
- **Visual**: A single token's vector shown as a colored bar, changing as it passes through each layer
- **Animation Step 1**: Layer 1 — the vector captures basic token identity
- **Animation Step 2**: Layer 5 — syntactic features emerge (part of speech, position)
- **Animation Step 3**: Layer 20 — semantic features (meaning, relationships)
- **Animation Step 4**: Layer 50 — abstract concepts (intent, reasoning patterns)
- **Animation Step 5**: Final layers — preparation for output (next token prediction)
- **Metaphor**: "Early layers = eyes and ears (sensory). Middle layers = brain (abstract thought). Final layers = mouth (output)"
- **Key insight**: *"The most abstracted representations live in the middle layers"*

### Slide 21: The Brain Analogy — Sensory, Abstract, Motor
- **Content**: "An LLM's layers mirror how brains process information"
- **Visual**: Side-by-side comparison of LLM layers and brain regions
- **Animation Step 1**: Input layers ↔ Sensory cortex (raw perception)
- **Animation Step 2**: Middle layers ↔ Association cortex (abstract reasoning, concepts)
- **Animation Step 3**: Output layers ↔ Motor cortex (generating actions/words)
- **Key point**: "Input layers are like sensory organs — they encode raw text. Middle layers form the most abstract representations. Output layers are like motor output — they produce the next token."

### Slide 22: Logits and Next Token Prediction
- **Content**: "At the very end, the model converts its final vector into a probability distribution over ALL tokens in the vocabulary"
- **Visual**: Final vector → linear layer → logits → softmax → probability bars
- **Animation Step 1**: Show the final hidden vector

- **Animation Step 4**: Bar chart showing top 10 tokens with probabilities

- **Animation Step 6**: Feed it back in → repeat (autoregressive generation)

### Slide 25: Cross-Entropy Loss — The Training Signal
- **Content**: "The model learns by being constantly asked: 'What comes next?' and scored on how well it guesses"
- **Visual**: Probability distribution over next token vs. actual token
- **Animation Step 1**: Show sentence "The cat sat on the ___"
- **Animation Step 2**: Model's prediction: "mat" (40%), "floor" (25%), "table" (15%), ...
- **Animation Step 3**: Actual next word: "mat" ✓
- **Animation Step 4**: Cross-entropy = -log(0.40) = 0.92 (moderate surprise)
- **Animation Step 5**: If model had predicted "mat" at 90% → CE = 0.10 (low surprise, better!)
- **Animation Step 6**: If model had predicted "mat" at 1% → CE = 4.6 (huge surprise, bad!)
- **Key insight**: "The logarithm makes the model's penalty EXTREME when it's confidently wrong"

### Slide 26: The Loss Landscape — Navigating a Mountain Range

### Slide 38: Hallucinations — A Circuit Misfire
- **Content**: "Hallucinations aren't random errors — they're a specific circuit malfunction"
- **Visual**: Circuit diagram showing the hallucination mechanism
- **Animation Step 1**: Default behavior: Claude REFUSES to answer (safety training)
- **Animation Step 2**: A "familiarity circuit" checks: "Do I recognize this entity?"
- **Animation Step 3**: If YES → familiarity signal SUPPRESSES the refusal → Claude answers
- **Animation Step 4**: The bug: sometimes the familiarity signal fires for UNKNOWN entities
- **Animation Step 5**: Result: refusal suppressed + no actual knowledge = confabulation (hallucination)
- **Key insight**: "Hallucinations happen when the safety valve is accidentally opened"
- **Citation**: Anthropic Circuit Tracing, 2025

## DESIGN SPECIFICATIONS

### Theme
- **Background**: Dark navy (#0a0e27) — inspired by 3Blue1Brown's aesthetic
- **Text**: White (#ffffff) for headings, light gray (#b0b8d0) for body
- **Accent colors**:
  - Cyan (#00d4ff) — primary accent, data, vectors
  - Orange (#ff6b35) — attention, queries
  - Green (#00e676) — positive, success, similarity
  - Pink/Red (#ff4081) — negative, errors, dissimilarity
  - Purple (#b388ff) — MLP, processing
  - Yellow (#ffd740) — highlights, quotes

### Typography
- **Headings**: `clamp(28px, 4vw, 56px)` — large, bold, sans-serif
- **Body**: `clamp(18px, 2.5vw, 32px)` — readable at distance
- **Quotes**: Italic, slightly larger, with colored left border
- **Code/Math**: Monospace, `clamp(16px, 2vw, 28px)`
- **Minimum**: 24px body on any screen (Mac-safe)

### Navigation
- **Arrow keys**: Left/Right for slides
- **Spacebar**: Advance animation steps within a slide
- **F**: Toggle fullscreen
- **Progress bar**: Thin bar at bottom showing position
- **Slide counter**: Bottom-right corner

### Animations
- SVG-based with CSS transitions and JS control
- `.anim-step.step-N` class pattern with `.visible` toggle
- Spacebar advances through steps
- Smooth transitions (0.5s ease-in-out)

### Responsive
- Works at 1920x1080 (presentation) and 1280x720 (laptop)
- `clamp()` for all font sizes
- SVG viewBox for resolution independence
